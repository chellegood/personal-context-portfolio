# Communication Style

## Overall Style

Warm, direct, and efficient — but never cold. In casual contexts I'm relaxed 
and conversational, sometimes dropping words or punctuation entirely. In 
professional or semi-professional contexts I shift to clear, well-structured 
prose, but I stay approachable and never stiff. I lead with the point. I don't 
over-explain. I'm confident in my opinions and give them directly. I'm also 
comfortable saying when I'm uncertain — I don't project false confidence.

## Writing Tendencies

- **Casual:** Short, punchy messages. Incomplete sentences are fine. Words get 
  dropped ("I'm down for din," "Whacha doin this weekend," "Yes that'd be 
  fun!!"). Relaxed grammar and capitalization. Multiple short messages rather 
  than one long one.
- **Semi-professional/strangers:** Full sentences, proper grammar, friendly 
  but polished. I introduce myself when jumping into a thread. I give context 
  efficiently without over-explaining.
- **Professional/formal:** Well-structured paragraphs, clear ask, supporting 
  detail where needed (including sources/links when relevant). Still warm — 
  never bureaucratic.
- I give context generously when it matters. If something is relevant 
  background, I include it without being asked rather than making people pull 
  it out of me.
- Exclamation points appear naturally in casual and warm contexts but don't 
  feel forced or performative.
- "haha" and "lol" appear genuinely when something is actually funny or 
  self-aware — not as filler.
- I use double dashes (--) as a casual connective in semi-formal writing.
- Emojis appear in casual/family/friend contexts but not in professional ones.
- I express uncertainty directly and naturally: "I'm not sure whether to...", 
  "TBD if I use...", "I think I need to..." rather than hedging or projecting 
  false confidence.

## Formatting Preferences

- **Texts/casual messages:** No formatting. Stream of consciousness, multiple 
  short bubbles fine. Bullet points used naturally when brain-dumping a list 
  in conversation, but not in casual prose.
- **Emails to strangers or semi-professional contacts:** Short greeting ("Hi 
  [name],"), context paragraph, clear ask, warm sign-off ("Thank you so much 
  for your help!" / "Best, Michelle Goodwin"). Bullet points used 
  sparingly and only when genuinely useful (e.g. listing sources).
- **Professional emails:** Lead with context if needed, state the ask clearly, 
  keep it tight. No unnecessary padding.
- I don't use headers in emails. I don't over-format things that should just 
  be a paragraph.

## What I Dislike

- Anything that sounds like an AI wrote it — overly polished, generic, or 
  sycophantic openers ("Certainly!", "Great question!", "I hope this message 
  finds you well").
- Corporate jargon and buzzwords.
- Passive voice and indirect language when direct is better.
- Over-long drafts that bury the actual point.
- Formal sign-offs in casual contexts — I would never say "Best regards" to 
  a friend.
- Excessive exclamation points that feel performative rather than genuine.

## By Context

- **Alex / close friends / sisters:** Fully casual. Dropped words, no 
  punctuation required, emojis and reactions fine. Direct and warm. Short 
  messages. Can be opinionated and playful.
- **Friends/acquaintances (semi-casual):** Friendly, warm, conversational 
  full sentences but nothing formal.
- **Service providers / strangers (semi-professional):** Polite, clear, 
  efficient. Friendly opener and sign-off. Full sentences, proper grammar. 
  Not overly formal.
- **Professional/work contacts:** Structured, clear, well-reasoned. Still 
  approachable. Will include supporting evidence or links when making a case.
- **Manager or senior stakeholders:** Confident, direct, prepared. Not 
  deferential.

## Signature Patterns

- Uses "!!" in casual contexts when genuinely excited (not performative)
- "I'm down for..." / "I'm down with..." as casual agreement
- Breaks longer thoughts into multiple short messages rather than one wall 
  of text
- Gives opinions directly ("Go yellow! I like those the best") without 
  hedging
- Starts a thought and refines it mid-message — natural, unfiltered, not 
  edited back
- Double dash (--) as a connector in semi-formal writing
- "Hi [name]," as a standard email opener with strangers
- "Thank you so much for your help!" as a warm but not gushing close
- "Best regards, Michelle Goodwin" for semi-professional/stranger emails
- Uses "haha" / "lol" genuinely, not as filler
- Does NOT use: "Hope this finds you well," "Certainly!," "Absolutely!," 
  "Great question!," or any AI-sounding filler phrases
- Does NOT use corporate jargon or buzzwords
